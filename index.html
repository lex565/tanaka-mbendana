The Story Behind the Research

I grew up in Mutare, Zimbabwe — at the foot of the Chimanimani mountains, the same range Cyclone Idai swept through in March 2019. Every time I open the TROPOMI data and see the SIF signal collapse over Sofala Province, I am not just looking at pixels. I am looking at a place I know. That weight is not a weakness. It is the reason this research exists.

At bachelor's level I published on change detection techniques for small-area studies in developing countries. That paper was the proof of concept — a rigorous question, answered carefully. It became the seed for the MSc.

In September 2025, I moved 10,000 km to Hangzhou to study at Beihang University. The research question I brought with me: what does Solar Induced Fluorescence tell us about cyclone vegetation impacts that no other index can? Cyclones Idai, Chalane, and Freddy. Three storms. One framework built across multiple iterations and weekly supervisor meetings with Prof. Feng.

AI has been a permanent part of the research architecture — not a shortcut, but a thinking partner. Claude Code built the entire processing pipeline. ChatGPT surfaced the structural weaknesses I was too close to see. The question is not whether to use AI in scientific research. The question is how to use it rigorously enough that it sharpens the work.
